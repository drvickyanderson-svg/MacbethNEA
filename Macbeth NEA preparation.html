Essay Structure

A suggested structure for a 1000–1500 word Task A essay on gender roles in Macbeth.

💡
The Task A question is always framed around Continuity and Change: you must discuss how attitudes to gender are presented and how a modern audience might respond to them — the same or differently?
Sample Task A Question (Gender)

Explore how attitudes to gender are presented in Macbeth and how these attitudes are different or the same for a modern audience.

1
Introduction (approx. 100 words)
Introduce the play's central attitudes to gender. State your argument: were Jacobean gender roles more rigid than today? Acknowledge complexity — Lady Macbeth both conforms to and subverts expectations. Signal what your essay will cover.
Shakespeare wrote Macbeth in 1606 for a Jacobean audience in which gender roles were strictly defined — men as warriors and rulers, women as passive and domestic. Yet Shakespeare complicates these expectations through characters like Lady Macbeth, creating a play that continues to challenge audiences today.
2
Paragraph 1 — Lady Macbeth's subversion of gender
How does Shakespeare present Lady Macbeth challenging gender norms? Analyse specific language. Then compare: how might a modern audience view her differently to a Jacobean one?
Focus on: "unsex me here" / "Come, you spirits" — the imperative verbs show her seeking to reject feminine qualities. A modern audience may admire her ambition; a Jacobean audience would likely be horrified by her defiance of God's natural order.
3
Paragraph 2 — Macbeth and masculine identity
How is Macbeth pressured by ideas of masculinity? Lady Macbeth and the witches both manipulate him through his gender identity. Continuity/Change: do modern audiences still recognise the pressure to "be a man"?
Focus on: "When you durst do it, then you were a man" — Lady Macbeth weaponises masculine identity. Modern audiences may recognise this as toxic masculinity, a concept with continuity into the 21st century.
4
Paragraph 3 — The witches and gender transgression
The witches blur gender boundaries. Banquo says they "should be women, / And yet your beards forbid me to interpret / That you are so." How does Shakespeare use the witches to unsettle the gender binary?
A Jacobean audience would associate bearded women with the supernatural and evil. A modern audience may be more comfortable with gender ambiguity, yet may still find the witches' power threatening — just for different reasons.
5
Paragraph 4 — Lady Macbeth's collapse and gender
By Act 5, Lady Macbeth is broken. Does Shakespeare punish her for stepping outside her gender role? Explore the sleepwalking scene and what it suggests about female ambition.
Focus on: "Out, damned spot!" — the repetition reflects a fragmented mind. Is this Shakespeare suggesting women cannot sustain power? A modern audience might find this disappointing; a Jacobean audience may have felt order was restored.
6
Conclusion (approx. 100 words)
Bring your argument together. Make a clear judgement about continuity and change: which attitudes have changed most? Which remain surprisingly relevant? Avoid introducing new points.
Overall, Shakespeare presents gender as both a social construction and a source of power and destruction. While a modern audience may reject Jacobean ideas of female passivity, the play's exploration of how gender is performed — and policed — remains strikingly relevant today.

Macduff · Act 5 Scene 8
"Macduff was from his mother's womb / Untimely ripped"
This is one of the most charged moments in the play for gender analysis. Macduff reveals he was delivered by what we would now call a caesarean section — he was literally cut from his mother's body rather than born naturally. This means he never truly passed through the female body in the conventional sense, and therefore technically fulfils the witches' prophecy that "none of woman born shall harm Macbeth."

The implication is deeply unsettling: the only man capable of defeating Macbeth — a king who has been corrupted and destroyed by female influence (the witches, Lady Macbeth) — is a man who has been separated from the female body entirely. Shakespeare seems to suggest that to undo the damage done by women, you need a man who was barely touched by womanhood at all. A modern audience may find this logic troubling, even misogynistic — it implies that female influence is itself a kind of contamination. A Jacobean audience, however, may have read it as the restoration of a properly masculine, divinely ordained order.

For Band 5, consider: does this reinforce or undermine the idea that Shakespeare is simply reflecting Jacobean gender norms? Or is he exposing the extremity — and fragility — of patriarchal logic?

Witches · Act 1 Scene 1
"Fair is foul, and foul is fair"
The witches open the play by inverting moral and natural order. This choral line — spoken together, in unison — establishes them as a collective force outside normal categories. In gender terms, they represent everything that disrupts the natural hierarchy: they are female but bearded, powerful but marginalised, prophetic but demonic. Their inversion of "fair" and "foul" mirrors the inversion of gender norms that drives the whole play. A Jacobean audience would immediately associate this chaos with the danger of female power unchecked. A modern audience might instead read the witches as figures who reveal that "natural" order is itself a construction — something imposed, not given.

Lady Macbeth · Act 1 Scene 5
"Come to my woman's breasts / And take my milk for gall"
Lady Macbeth invites the spirits to replace her breast milk — the ultimate symbol of maternal femininity and nurture — with gall (bitter poison). This is a violent rejection of the body's most gendered function. In Jacobean society, breastfeeding was seen as a sacred female duty; to ask for it to be corrupted is to reject womanhood at its most fundamental. The physical imagery makes her transgression visceral and shocking. A modern audience might read this as a woman violently refusing to be reduced to her reproductive role — but Shakespeare frames it as an act of self-corruption that ultimately destroys her.

Macbeth · Act 3 Scene 4
"What man dare, I dare... / The armed rhinoceros, or the Hyrcan tiger"
Confronted by Banquo's ghost, Macbeth desperately tries to reassert his masculine identity through a list of dangerous animals he would fearlessly face. The hyperbolic imagery — rhinoceros, tiger — reveals the performative nature of his masculinity. He is not genuinely courageous; he is performing courage because the ghost has unmanned him in front of his court. The irony is sharp: a man who murdered his king and his friend in cold blood cannot face a ghost. Shakespeare suggests that the kind of violent, performative masculinity Macbeth has pursued is hollow — it does not produce real strength. A modern audience might read this as a deconstruction of toxic masculinity.

Lady Macbeth · Act 2 Scene 2
"My hands are of your colour; but I shame / To wear a heart so white"
After the murder of Duncan, Lady Macbeth scorns Macbeth's guilt, calling his pale ("white") heart shameful — cowardly. White here is associated with femininity and weakness; red (blood, action) with masculinity. She is again using gender as a weapon, shaming him for emotional responses she codes as female. Yet the dramatic irony is devastating: by Act 5, it is Lady Macbeth herself who cannot escape the blood she now sees on her hands. The colour symbolism comes full circle — her attempts to suppress feminine guilt ultimately destroy her. A modern audience might find this one of the play's most powerful ironies.

Continuity & Change

The heart of this unit. You must compare Jacobean attitudes with how a modern audience might respond.

⚠️
Context should inform your analysis — not replace it. Don't write long paragraphs about Jacobean society without linking back to Shakespeare's language and a modern perspective. The mark scheme rewards context that shapes meaning.
Key Jacobean Context — Gender

In 1606, gender roles were understood as divinely ordained. Men were expected to be rational, active and dominant; women passive, domestic and obedient. A woman who sought power was seen as unnatural and linked to witchcraft. The "Great Chain of Being" placed men above women as part of God's natural order. Lady Macbeth's behaviour would have been deeply unsettling to a Jacobean audience.

⚡ Don't oversimplify — this context is more complicated

It would be too simple to say "Jacobeans would never accept female power." In fact, England had just lived through 45 years of Queen Elizabeth I's reign (1558–1603) — a woman who wielded enormous political authority and was celebrated for it. Jacobean audiences in 1606 had real, recent experience of powerful female rule.

So why does Macbeth present female power so negatively? Shakespeare was writing for King James I, who came to the throne in 1603. James was deeply anxious to assert his own legitimacy as a male monarch — partly because of Elizabeth's reign. He was also famously obsessed with witchcraft, having written his own book on the subject (Daemonologie, 1597). Shakespeare's negative portrayal of powerful women — Lady Macbeth, the witches — can be read as a deliberate piece of political flattery, affirming James's belief that male kingship was the natural and God-given order.

This means a Band 5 response won't just say "Jacobeans feared female power." It will acknowledge that attitudes were contested and politicised — that Shakespeare was making a specific political argument for a specific king, not simply reflecting universal Jacobean belief. Some audience members may even have found Lady Macbeth's intelligence and ambition compelling, not just horrifying.

Use this in your essay like this:

"While it might seem that a Jacobean audience would straightforwardly reject Lady Macbeth's ambition, the reality is more complex. Having lived through Elizabeth I's reign, many audience members had direct experience of powerful female rule. Shakespeare's negative portrayal of female power may be less a reflection of universal Jacobean attitudes and more a calculated act of political flattery towards James I, who wrote extensively about the dangers of witchcraft and the divine right of male kings."

Shakespeare uses Lady Macbeth to reveal how masculine identity in Jacobean society was weaponised — used to shame men into violence. In Act 1 Scene 7, she taunts him: "When you durst do it, then you were a man." The conditional structure of "when you durst do it" implies that killing Duncan was not merely ambition but a test of manhood — and one Macbeth has already failed by hesitating. Lady Macbeth weaponises his masculine identity against his conscience. A modern audience is likely to recognise this as an example of toxic masculinity — the same cultural script that tells men they must suppress doubt, emotion and ethics to prove their strength. Far from feeling dated, this exchange has striking continuity with 21st-century expectations of men.
